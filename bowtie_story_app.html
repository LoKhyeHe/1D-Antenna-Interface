Meet Wi-Fi Wayne.
He will not stop talking to your pants.

A 2.45 GHz bedtime story about routers, rats, and one very determined bow-tie.

📡

Wayne is your Wi-Fi router. Wayne loves you. Wayne broadcasts at 2.45 GHz, 24/7, and Wayne does not care that your phone is in your pocket right next to, well… the family jewels.

🐀

Scientists already zapped some very unlucky lab rats with 2.45 GHz at close range. Result: more DNA damage, worse sperm quality, sad rats. Nobody is volunteering humans for that experiment — ethics boards get twitchy about "please irradiate this man's testes for science."

🕵️

So instead of guessing from "how many hours a day do you use your phone" surveys (the current best human proxy — not exactly lab-grade), we want a tiny, comfortable, wearable sensor that sits near the body and actually measures the real RF dose, quietly, without anyone changing their behaviour.

The Plan To sense 2.45 GHz RF, you need… an antenna. Not a bulky router antenna — a flat, flexible, skin-friendly one. Enter the bow-tie: two triangular metal wings that are famously good at soaking up energy over a wide band, easy to print flat, and small enough to disappear into a wearable patch.
🎛️

But a bow-tie has a personality problem: change its flare angle or its size, and its favourite frequency (and how well it "grabs" Wayne's signal) shifts around. Too narrow a flare and it's basically a stick. Too wide and it forgets how to match impedance. There's a sweet spot — and it's your job to find it.

🎯

Your mission, should you choose to accept it: drag the sliders below until the antenna resonates right at 2.45 GHz with a nice deep S11 dip (below −10 dB is the pro move). That's the shape we'd actually cut out of copper or EGaIn liquid metal and put on a body.
