Meet Wi-Fi Wayne.

A 2.45 GHz story about routers, rats, and the bow-tie sent to investigate.

📡

Wayne is always talking.

Your router (or phone, in your pocket) broadcasts 2.45 GHz Wi-Fi, all day, every day. Question: how close does Wayne usually get to your body?

Reality Check Doesn't matter which you picked — modern life means near-continuous, body-proximate RF exposure. Phones in pockets, laptops on laps, routers indoors. Wayne doesn't respect personal space.
🐀

Somewhere, a lab rat had a bad week.

Researchers exposed rats to 2.45 GHz at close range (router-like power density) for a controlled time-course study. What do you think happened?

Reality Check Option 3 is what the literature actually found (Shokri et al., 2015): increased germ-cell apoptosis, and falling sperm count, motility & viability, worsening the longer the exposure ran. Humans obviously can't ethically be tested the same way — the best we have is indirect, self-reported phone-use surveys.
🕵️

So... what do we actually do about it?

We can't ethically replicate the rat study on humans. So what's the move?

The Mission Option 3. Ethics boards (rightly) veto direct-harm human testing, and "hours of phone use" is an indirect, confounded proxy with no measured field data behind it. The gap: harm is shown in animals, only inferred in humans. To close it, we need a flexible, wearable, 2.45 GHz-sensitive antenna that can sit near the body and measure exposure directly.

A flat, printable bow-tie is the natural shape for the job — wideband, easy to fabricate flat, small enough to disappear into a wearable patch. Now it's your turn to design one.
